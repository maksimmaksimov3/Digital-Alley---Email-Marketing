Part 0102 · System
What runs the program

Four pieces. In this order.

Piece
01
The calendar
Seven fields per send: date, topic, owner, status, subject line, draft link, send list. If you cannot fill them, the email is not ready.
Piece
02
The content mix
Five types rotated: method, story, resource roundup, product depth, soft offer. Ratio: 80 percent value, 20 percent sales.
Piece
03
The four-week rhythm
Week 1 method. Week 2 story. Week 3 resource. Week 4 offer. Then start again. The team always knows what is next.
Piece
04
Evidence first. AI second.
Real winners, past CTAs, sales objections, customer questions go in first. Then AI ideates on top. Reverse the order and you ship beautiful generic.

Same team. Same number of sends. Different system. We have seen open rate go from 18 percent to mid-30s in five months on this rebuild alone.

Part 0203 · Segmentation
The frame that survives a small list

Five dimensions. Use only what drives different content.

Dimension
01
Application
Microscopy. Quantum optics. Telecom. Mechanobiology. Test & measurement. Application fit beats everything else for most photonics buyers.
Dimension
02
Role
Researcher, procurement, engineering manager, technical CTO, distributor. Same email. Different first paragraph.
Dimension
03
Journey stage
Awareness, consideration, decision, customer, advocate. Same person can sit in different stages for different product lines.
Dimension
04
Source
Web form. Trade show scan. LinkedIn. Webinar. Sales-added. Wildly different intent. A scan deserves a different first email than a referral.
Dimension
05
Engagement
Opened, clicked, dormant, cold. The single most powerful segment in your whole list, and the one most companies do not actively use.

Not all five are filled in for every contact. That is fine. Use what you have.

Part 0304 · Personalization
The four levers

From easiest to hardest. From broadest to most powerful.

Lever 01 · Firmographic
01
Company shape
Size, region, industry. Lab vs integrator vs research institution. Same product. Different first paragraph.
Lever 02 · Technographic
02
Their setup
Detector, light source, control software, standards they have to meet. Lets the email read like a peer wrote it.
Lever 03 · Behavioral
03
What they did
Opened, clicked, downloaded, visited the pricing page. Most underused source in photonics. Easiest to start collecting.
Lever 04 · Intent
04
Buying right now
Two pricing-page visits this week. Three opens on one thread. New contact at the same company appears. Faster, more sales-oriented next step.

Start with firmographic plus behavioral. You are already ahead of 90 percent of the photonics industry.

When you let AI draft for a technical buyer

The humanization checklist. Perfect timing.

Almost everyone is using AI to draft now. This is how you keep the final email from sounding generic.

Action 01
01
Strip the AI tells
No "unlock", "leverage", "transform". No press-release rhythm. No three em dashes per sentence. If it sounds like a TED talk in an inbox, cut it.
Action 02
02
Add real specificity
The application. The product spec. The exact use case. AI writes generic. You add specific.
Action 03
03
Check for hallucinations
AI invents customer names, statistics, technical specs. Verify every concrete claim before send.
Action 04
04
Read it out loud
If you would not say it on a call, do not send it.
Action 05
05
One human review
Not for approval. For voice. Other humans catch what you cannot after an hour of staring at it.

Part 0405 · Automation
The flows that actually pay back

Build these four. Skip the rest until they run reliably.

Flow 01
01
Welcome
1 to 2 emails over 10 to 14 days. First one within 24 hours, while the request is still fresh. Re-use your past winners.
Flow 02
02
Nurture
2 to 4 emails over 4 to 6 weeks, behavior-branched. End cleanly. No fake one-to-one phrases on a sequence with no signal.
Flow 03
03
Trade show
Pre-show 3 to 4 weeks. Quiet during. Post-show within 48 hours for priority. The 6 weeks around the show are the campaign.
Flow 04
04
Webinar follow-up
Three audiences: attended, registered-not-shown, recording viewers. Three flows. One convergence point.

Part 0506 · CRM
What the CRM has to do for email

Three jobs. Nail these and the tool stops mattering.

Job 01
01
Source of truth for contact properties
Application tags, role, lifecycle stage, source, last engagement. If CRM and email platform disagree, you have a data problem. Fix it there first.
Job 02
02
Feed segments dynamically
No CSV exports. Segments live in the CRM. Email platform reads them in real time. A sync, not a one-way push.
Job 03
03
Track engagement back to contact
When sales looks at a prospect they should see the last 90 days of opens, clicks, content engaged. Without this, sales quietly stops trusting marketing data.

Part 0607 · Compliance
The country reality, simplified

Cold B2B email is country-specific. Plan by jurisdiction.

UK
01
Most relaxed market
Under PECR, corporate subscribers are mostly exempt from the consent rule. Email UK businesses with relative freedom. Easy unsubscribe required.
France
02
Reasonable for B2B
If the outreach is genuinely professional in context. Email them about their job, not personal interests. Right to object must be clear.
Germany
03
The hardest in Europe
UWG sets a very high bar for "presumed interest". Technically possible, brutal relevance test, real fines. Slow down and be precise.
Rest of EU
04
Country by country
Soft opt-in only for existing customers and similar goods. Does not cover new prospects. Check the local DPA before scaling outreach.

The 2026 deliverability stack

Beautiful emails go to spam when the plumbing is half-done.

Authentication
SPF
Sender Policy Framework. Records list authorized sending hosts.
Signing
DKIM
Cryptographic signature on every send. Must point to the current provider.
Policy
DMARC
Start at p=none. Progress to p=quarantine or p=reject within months.
Unsubscribe
1-click
RFC 8058 List-Unsubscribe headers. Required by Google, Yahoo, Microsoft.
Complaint rate
<0.3%
Enforcement kicks in here. Safe target below 0.1 percent.

Appendix · Likely questions

Q&A prep · short answers ready.

  1. We have a small list. Is it worth doing all this?
    The smaller the list, the more each email matters. A 500-contact list with a working welcome flow and a 4-week rhythm outperforms a 5,000-contact list that gets one Christmas card a year. Small list, high quality, high intent is exactly the case where this approach works best.
  2. We do not have HubSpot. Can we still do this?
    Yes. Brevo, ActiveCampaign, and even Mailchimp can do most of this. The tool matters less than the discipline. Pick a tool you will actually use, and stop platform shopping.
  3. How do you measure success?
    For technical B2B email, the four metrics that matter are: delivered rate, open rate, click rate, and meetings booked or leads progressed to sales. Everything else is noise.
  4. What about AI agents that write emails for me?
    They are useful for first drafts. They are dangerous for final sends. The humanization checklist is what keeps the final send usable. If you trust an AI agent to send unedited copy to a PhD audience, you will burn your sender reputation.
  5. How long until we see results?
    First improvements in open and click rates within 30 to 60 days. Meaningful lead-quality changes within 90 to 120 days. Compounding effects after 6 to 9 months.
  6. GDPR and cold email in Germany?
    Be very careful. Either work with a legal advisor or do not do cold outbound in Germany. The risk-to-reward ratio is bad for SMEs.
  7. Can we use ChatGPT to generate the welcome flow content?
    For draft. Not for final. Use real winners as the source content. AI helps with phrasing and tightening, not with deciding what gets sent.
  8. How often should we send to our main list?
    For technical B2B with a 500 to 5,000 contact list, every 2 weeks is a good baseline. Weekly if you have the content discipline. Monthly is too infrequent to maintain top-of-mind.
